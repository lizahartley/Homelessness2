This website aims to compile useful information for people to access in the event of their becoming homeless. It also is designed to provide easily accessible information for people wishing to learn more about how to help homeless people, where to volunteer and to bust myths about homelessness.
Here are some options for short term, emergency housing or longer term support which this website will set out in detail.

  • If you or someone you know are at risk of being made homeless, contact Streetlink on 0300 500 0914 or visit the Streetlink website
  • Contact the local council on: 01223 457918
  • If you are a single person, i.e you would not be accompanied by anyone else while sleeping rough, contact the citizens advice beureau: 01223 4567677

Long Term Support

Where to look for help with psychological support, finding work and accessing long term housing

  • Finding long term housing can be a long and frustrating process, but it is worth it. Here are some places to begin in finding support to get off the streets
  • Jimmy's holds seminar discussions on finding work every Friday at 8pm.

Jimmy's Feminine Health Project

Jimmy's Cambridge is a homelessness charity based on East Road. This year they have launched the Feminine Helth Project for female rough sleepers. The aim is to help women on the streets access sanitary products for free and to help rough sleepers avoid the health and wellbeing risks associated with homelessness.


Jimmy's and certain locations around the city, now provide free sanitary products for homeless women and peope of female bodies.

This map details where you can get free menstrual supplies and hand sanitizer. Check out below to find out why!

MAP OF FREE SANITARY PRODUCTS!

Having access to menstrual products gives homeless women back their dignity and independence, but it also protects from Toxic Shock Syndrome. Symptoms of Toxic Shock Syndrome (TSS) include:

  • the whites of the eyes, lips and tongue turning a bright red
  • a high temperature (fever) of 39C (102.2F) or above
  • flu-like symptoms, such as a headache, chills, muscle aches, a sore throat and a cough
  • sickness and diarrhoea

If you experience any of these symptoms dial the NHS hotline 111

Places to Stay

If you or someone you know finds themselves in need of emergency accomodation, there are a few options to follow:

  • Cambridge Churches Homelessness Project offers temporary accomodation on a rolling weekly basis. Click on the heart to see their timetable
  • Jimmy's Night shelter rarely has spaces availabel on the night, but if you go and ask at the front desk they can either arrange emergency indoor accomodation or they can put you on a waiting list to arrange it.

Information for Volunteers and the Public

If you or someone you know finds themselves in need of emergency accomodation, there are a few options to follow:

  • You don't always have to volunteer through an official organisation, just bringing hot drinks, blankets, food or just talking to homeless people can be a great help and comfort.
  • But if you do wish to volunteer then that's great news! Winter Comfort are currently pushing to take on volunteers for their meal service.
  • Jimmy's is always in need of volunteers, you can go and fill out the relevant forms at Jimmy's shelter on East Rd
  • Volunteering to help the homeless is a great and rewarding thing to do!

Mythbusting About Homelessness

Homeless people are sometimes stereotyped as all being dangerous, addicted to substances or too lazy to hold down a job. This is NOT TRUE. There are lots of reasons someone might become homeless

  • Myth #1: "People experiencing homelessness choose to be homeless" Most people become homeless because of economic circumstances, such as unemployment or the inability to afford rising living costs, according to a Homeless in Canada report. “It’s degrading and stigmatizing to be homeless,” says Dr. Stephen Gaetz, director of the Canadian Observatory on Homelessness. “There’s an incredible lack of safety, you’re more likely to be malnourished, you’re more likely to be a victim of crime, you’re more likely to be sexually assaulted, your health worsens, your mental health worsens. If you take a chronically homeless person [and] offer them housing with support, they’ll generally stay in housing.”
  • Myth #2: "It’s their own fault they’re homeless" While it’s hard to pinpoint a “cause” of homelessness, most people end up experiencing homelessness because of poverty, according to the Homeless in Canada report. And poverty is affected by complex issues such as lifelong trauma, family breakdown or mental health issues, according to Alex Cheng, client services director with Blue Door Shelters, a United Way-supported agency in York Region that provides emergency housing for youth, families and adult men. “We see a lot of folks that have a mental health diagnosis or use substances to self-medicate, and those are all barriers for individuals who are homeless to move forward.”
  • Myth #3: This would never happen to me" Homelessness can happen to anyone, A lot of people are one paycheque away from being homeless. … We see that a lot with families — because of finances or job loss or extenuating circumstances, the entire family becomes homeless.
    The first step in tackling unignorable issues like homelessness, is understanding them. Acknowledging that these myths are, in fact, myths, can help to reduce stigma, increase empathy and move people out of homelessness.
